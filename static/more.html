Why Baseline ML ?

In data science as in innovation in general, it is a very good practice to work in short iterations, building prototypes and simple products quickly instead of spending months or years building the perfect product.

Baseline ML helps you do that with machine learning models, providing the first models (often called baseline models) in a few minutes.

I expect Baseline ML to be primarily used by data scientist but I hope it can also be a simple discovery tool for anyone trying to leverage machine learning on their data.

How does it work ?

Let's suppose you have data on the SF bay area house rental market and you want to know quickly what kind of baseline performance you can expect when predicting prices.

First you need to upload your data as a CSV file. A CSV file is simply a table of your data, each column representing a variable, each row representing a data point:

Id,SquareFootage,City,MonthlyRent,YearlyRent
0,800,Palo Alto,3500,42000
1,1200,San Francisco,2900,34800
2,900,San Francisco,3000,36000
...

The variables can be separated by a comma or semi-colon or space. The file can be compressed, Baseline ML will try to understand it.

Then you have to choose the target. Baseline ML will present you the variables that it found in the dataset, and you will have to choose the one that you want to predict (the target). Here you may choose MonthlyRent as the target. You can also ignore variables that should not be used for prediction, because it would make no sense or because they are directly linked to the target. Here you should ignore Id and YearlyRent.

Finally you simply press run and wait for the results. Baseline ML will take the first 80% of your data to try a few baseline models. "nomodel" is barely a model, it predicts the average or most frequent value of the target, ignoring the other variables. It is still useful as a baseline, especially when you are trying to predict a rare event. "linear", "tree", and "forest" are classic models with a little preprocessing and very little tuning, they should provide a fast and decent baseline most of the time. After the tuning, the models are evaluated on the remaining 20% of the data. The performance on the last 20% is your baseline performance and is displayed. This is the kind of performance you can expect on this dataset, if it is satisfactory then it is great news, it should be easy to build a model at least as good. If it is disappointing try adding more meaningful variables to your dataset, like the neighborhood in our example. If it is extremelly good then make sure you ignored the variables directly linked to your target, like the YearlyRent. In any case, after you have done some exploration, talk to your data science team !

The performance will be shown in "mean absolute error", this is simply the error between the prediction and the reality, in absolute value and averaged over the test data points. If the target is in dollars, the performance will be in dollars.

You can also try and predict the city based on the rent. In this case the performance will be shown in accuracy, this is simply the success rate of the prediction on the test data points. The accuracy is a percentage, between 0 and 100%.

In any case you should have at least 100 data points, preferably 10 000 or more. Otherwise the results will have little credibility.
